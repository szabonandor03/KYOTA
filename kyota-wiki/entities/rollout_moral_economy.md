## Technical Core
The Moral Economy governs how the artist restructures the commercial relationship between themselves and the listener, bypassing institutional gatekeepers to establish a direct, high-trust exchange. KYOTA draws from three canonical implementations.

1. **Strategic Bifurcation (Radiohead — In Rainbows):** The "honesty box" pay-what-you-want model effectively legalized the artist's own leak, capturing the data and attention that would otherwise go to pirate sites. Harvard analysis confirmed zero negative impact on subsequent physical sales — "Digital Ubiquity" can serve as a promotional engine for "Physical Scarcity." The rollout must provide different tiers: a **low-friction digital entry** and a **high-touch physical experience** (the "newspaper album"). Critical rule: **Information Containment** — for a rollout to be disruptive, the circle of those who know must be as small as possible.

2. **The Contractual Heist (Frank Ocean — Endless/Blonde):** The "Decoy Drop" fulfills a legal obligation with an intentionally low-commercial work, freeing the canonical release for independent distribution at 70% revenue vs 14%. The 45-minute staircase livestream = a **filter for attention**, testing which fans will invest labor before the real reward arrives. The "Dual World" strategy (static/glitched Endless vs minimalist Blonde) forces fans to navigate two realities, deepening engagement.

3. **The Zenarchistic Hack (KLF — The Manual):** The pop charts are a "technical system" to be reverse-engineered, not a creative validation. Financial poverty is reframed as a **tactical advantage** — it forces hyper-efficient, committed decisions. "Negative World-Building" = creating mythos through the **destruction** of value (burning £1M, deleting the catalog). The orchestrator must be a **sociologist first** and a creator second.
